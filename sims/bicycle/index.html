Why a Bicycle Stays Up

In the spirit of Bartosz Ciechanowski's bicycle. The surprise: a moving bike balances itself, and the reason is the steering geometry — the front wheel trails behind the steering axis, so a lean makes it steer back underneath you. Canvas 2D · physics.

1 · Standing still, it falls

A bike is an upside-down pendulum. Give a stationary one the smallest lean and gravity just keeps pulling it over. Speed = 0 here — watch it topple.

2 · The secret is the geometry

Look at the side view (inset). The steering axis, extended down, hits the ground ahead of where the tire touches. That gap is the trail — and it changes everything.

3 · A trailing wheel self-aligns

Like a shopping-cart caster, a wheel dragged behind its pivot swings straight on its own. On a leaning bike, that same trail makes the front wheel flop into the lean.

4 · Steering into the lean rights it

Rolling forward, steering into the lean drives the wheels back underneath the center of mass — and the bike stands itself up. Faster makes this stronger (it scales with speed²).

5 · No trail, no magic

Set the trail to zero and the self-steering disappears. Even at speed, the same nudge now puts the bike on the ground. The geometry, not the gyroscope, is doing the work.
